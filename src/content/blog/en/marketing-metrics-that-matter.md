"The post got 5,000 likes!" — that sounds great, but the next question is the one that matters: **how many orders came from it?**

Marketing measurement is not about collecting as many numbers as possible. It is about choosing **the few numbers that tell you what to do next**.

## Vanity metrics vs. actionable metrics

**Vanity metrics** look impressive but lead to no decision:
- Impressions, likes, follower count
- Total website visits

**Actionable metrics** tell you what to fix:
- Conversion rate at each step
- Customer acquisition cost (CAC)
- Return / repeat purchase rate

> A quick test: ask yourself *"If this number doubled, what would I do differently?"* If you cannot answer, it is a vanity metric.

## Pick metrics based on your goal

Do not track everything. Each stage needs only a few core numbers:

| Goal | Primary metric | Secondary metric |
|---|---|---|
| Awareness | New reach | Video completion rate |
| Traffic | Sessions, traffic sources | Bounce rate, time on page |
| Lead generation | Leads, cost per lead | Form completion rate |
| Sales | Revenue, ROAS, CAC | Average order value |
| Retention | Repeat purchase rate | Purchase frequency, LTV |

## Three foundational numbers everyone should know

**1. CAC — Customer Acquisition Cost**
> CAC = Total marketing & sales spend ÷ Number of new customers

**2. AOV — Average Order Value**
> AOV = Total revenue ÷ Number of orders

**3. LTV — Lifetime Value**
> LTV = AOV × Average purchase count × Customer lifespan

The survival rule: **LTV must exceed CAC** — ideally by 3× or more. If you spend $8 to acquire a customer who buys once for $6, you lose money on every sale, even while your revenue report climbs.

## Build a minimal report

You do not need a complex dashboard. A spreadsheet with five columns, updated weekly, is enough:

| Week | Spend | Leads/Orders | Cost per result | Revenue |
|---|---|---|---|---|

Look at this table for eight consecutive weeks and you will see trends more clearly than in any elaborate report.

**Free tools worth having:**
- **Google Analytics 4** — website behavior
- **Google Search Console** — SEO performance
- Each platform's own ads manager
- Google Sheets to consolidate everything in one place

## Read data to make decisions

Numbers only mean something when **compared**:

- **Against the previous period** — is this month better than last?
- **Against the target** — what percentage of plan are you hitting?
- **Across channels** — which channel gives the lowest cost per result?

A simple diagnostic process when results are poor:

1. Did traffic drop? → the problem is your **acquisition channel**
2. Traffic is fine but few leads? → the problem is your **landing page or offer**
3. Many leads but few orders? → the problem is **pricing or the sales process**
4. Selling but not profitable? → the problem is **CAC or margin**

This helps you fix the right thing instead of blindly raising the budget.

## 3 traps when reading data

1. **Concluding from too small a sample** — 10 clicks is not enough to say creative A beats B.
2. **Confusing correlation with causation** — revenue rising while you run ads does not mean ads caused all of it (it could be seasonal).
3. **Only looking at totals** — total revenue may rise while one channel bleeds money underneath.

## Start today

Pick the **three metrics** that matter most to your current goal, put them in a spreadsheet, and update them every Monday.

Measurement is not about having lots of data — it is about **looking regularly at a few right numbers** and being willing to change when they tell you the truth.
