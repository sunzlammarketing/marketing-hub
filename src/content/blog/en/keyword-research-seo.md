The classic beginner SEO mistake: pick the hottest keyword in your industry, write a great article, then wait forever for a ranking that never comes.

The reason is simple: those keywords are held by large sites with thousands of backlinks. A new website needs a different strategy — **come in through the back door**.

## Understanding long-tail keywords

Keywords fall into three groups by length and specificity:

| Type | Example | Search volume | Competition | Conversion |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Head | "running shoes" | Very high | Very high | Low |
| Mid | "men's running shoes" | High | High | Medium |
| Long-tail | "running shoes for heavier runners" | Low | Low | **High** |

> A useful paradox: the fewer people searching a term, the closer those searchers are to buying. Someone typing "running shoes for heavier runners" is genuinely about to purchase.

## 5 free sources for keyword ideas

**1. Google autocomplete**
Type your main keyword then add a, b, c... one at a time. Google suggests dozens of variations real people search.

**2. "People also ask"**
The question box in the middle of the results page. Each question is a potential article title.

**3. "Related searches"**
At the bottom of Google's results — an excellent source for supporting articles.

**4. Google Search Console**
If your site is already live, open the Performance report. Look for keywords where you sit at **positions 8-20** — light optimization often pushes them to page one.

**5. Customer comments and messages**
Customers use the same everyday language they type into Google.

## Judge difficulty by eye

You do not need a paid tool. Search the keyword and look at the top 10 results:

- All major news sites and big marketplaces → **very hard**, skip it
- Forums, personal blogs, Q&A pages present → **there is an opening**, go for it
- Results that do not actually answer the question → **great opportunity**, you can do better

This is the most practical assessment, and it costs nothing.

## Group keywords by search intent

Once you have a list, sort by what the searcher wants:

- **Wants to know**: "what is seo", "how to run ads" → write a guide
- **Wants to compare**: "shopee vs lazada" → write a comparison
- **Wants to buy**: "seo service pricing" → write a service page

A common mistake is pointing a sales page at a "wants to know" keyword. Google will not rank it, because the content does not match the intent.

## The content cluster strategy

Do not write disconnected articles. Build clusters instead:

- **1 pillar article** targeting a mid-tail keyword: "SEO Guide for Beginners"
- **5-8 supporting articles** targeting long-tail keywords: "how to choose keywords", "optimizing title tags", "writing meta descriptions"...
- Supporting articles **link back to the pillar**, and the pillar links out to them

This structure signals topical depth to Google, and the articles lift each other in the rankings.

## What to do this week

Pick **one** topic you know best. Use Google autocomplete to find 10 long-tail keywords around it.

Write one article for the **least competitive** keyword on that list. Ranking for one small keyword beats never ranking for a big one.
