Many small businesses make the same mistake: trying to sell to **everyone**. The result is a generic message, expensive advertising, and customers who never feel "this is made for me".

**STP** — Segmentation, Targeting, Positioning — is the model that fixes this at the root, before you even think about running ads or writing content.

## S — Segmentation: Split the market into groups

Instead of seeing the market as one block, split it into groups with different traits and needs. Four common approaches:

| Approach | Example |
|---|---|
| **Demographic** | Age, gender, income, occupation |
| **Geographic** | Urban vs. rural, north vs. south |
| **Behavioral** | Purchase frequency, loyalty, buying occasion |
| **Psychographic** | Lifestyle, values, concerns |

> Segmenting by **behavior and psychographics** usually beats demographics. Two 30-year-olds can buy for completely different reasons.

## T — Targeting: Pick the group worth pursuing

Not every group deserves investment. Filter with four questions:

1. **Is it big enough?** Are there enough people to build sustainable revenue?
2. **Do they have money and decision power?** They both want to buy and can buy.
3. **Can you reach them?** Is there a channel that reaches them at reasonable cost?
4. **Can you serve them better than competitors?** This is the deciding factor.

With limited resources, **picking one group and serving it excellently** always beats spreading across three.

## P — Positioning: Own a space in their mind

Positioning is not what you say about yourself — it is **what customers think when they hear your name**.

A one-sentence positioning framework:

> For **[your chosen group]**, **[brand]** is the **[category]** that helps **[distinctive benefit]**, because **[reason to believe]**.

A concrete example:

> For online clothing shop owners, *Kho Ảnh Sạch* is a product photography service that delivers marketplace-ready photos within 24 hours, because the studio sits right at the wholesale market.

Good positioning always involves a **trade-off**. If your positioning statement is equally true for every competitor, it is not positioning yet.

## A worked example: a small coffee shop

- **Segmentation**: students studying for exams · office workers meeting quickly · freelancers who need to sit for hours
- **Targeting**: choose the **freelancer** group — higher spend, longer stay, more repeat visits
- **Positioning**: "The coffee shop for people who work all day — power outlets at every table, strong wifi, no time limit"

From that positioning, every other decision becomes obvious: comfortable long-sitting furniture, quiet music, a lunch menu, content aimed at freelancers.

## 3 common mistakes

1. **Segmenting but never choosing** — segments exist on paper, yet ads still target everyone.
2. **Picking a group that is too broad** — "women 25-45" is not a segment, it is half the population.
3. **Positioning with words everyone uses** — "quality", "trusted", "good price" create no differentiation.

## Start today

Take a piece of paper and write down the **three customer groups** you currently serve, then circle the one that brings the best profit with the least hassle.

For the next 30 days, write content and run ads **for that group only**. You will be surprised how much results improve while your budget stays the same.
