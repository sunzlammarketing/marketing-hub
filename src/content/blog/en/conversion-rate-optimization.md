There are two ways to grow revenue: bring in more people, or **sell to more of the people already arriving**. The first costs money; the second costs thought.

If 1,000 people visit each month and 1% buy, you get 10 orders. Lift that to 2% and you get **20 orders — double, on the same ad spend**. That is why conversion rate optimization (CRO) is usually worth doing before increasing budget.

## First: measure where you stand

The basic formula:

> Conversion rate = (People who took the action ÷ Total visitors) × 100

But do not measure only the final number. Measure **each step**:

| Step | Example rate |
|---|---|
| Landed → viewed product | 40% |
| Viewed product → added to cart | 15% |
| Added to cart → started checkout | 50% |
| Started checkout → completed | 60% |

Whichever step is **unusually low** compared to the others is the leak to fix first.

## 8 common leaks

**1. Slow page load**
Every extra second of waiting loses a share of visitors. Compressing images and removing unnecessary scripts is the cheapest high-impact fix.

**2. Unclear what you sell in the first 5 seconds**
A stranger must immediately understand: what this is, who it is for, and the main benefit.

**3. Weak call-to-action button**
The button must stand out, sit somewhere obvious, and state the action ("Get a quote" beats "Submit").

**4. Forms that are too long**
Every required field is another excuse to leave. Ask only for what you genuinely need to fulfil the order.

**5. Missing trust signals**
Real reviews, customer photos, units sold, return policy — place them right next to the buy button.

**6. Surprise costs at the last step**
Shipping fees appearing at checkout is a leading cause of cart abandonment. State them upfront.

**7. Forced account creation**
Always offer a guest checkout option.

**8. Poor mobile experience**
Most customers buy on their phone. Small text, cramped buttons, pinch-to-zoom — you lose them instantly.

## Running A/B tests properly

Once you have a hypothesis, test it instead of guessing:

1. **Change one thing at a time** — changing the headline and the button together tells you nothing about which mattered.
2. **Run it long enough** — at least 1-2 weeks and enough visits that the result is not random noise.
3. **Prioritize high-impact elements** — headline, price and offer matter far more than button color.

> Do not test button colors while the page still takes 8 seconds to load. Always fix the big problem first.

## Where do hypotheses come from?

Data tells you **where** the leak is, not **why**. To learn why:

- Watch session recordings (Microsoft Clarity is free) to see where people get stuck
- Add a single survey question: "What almost stopped you from buying?"
- Re-read your inbox: repeated questions signal information missing from the page

## Start this week

Open your sales page **on a phone**, pretend to be a stranger, and go all the way through the purchase flow.

Write down every point where you had to pause, think, or felt annoyed. That list is your to-do — and there are usually at least three things you can fix today.
