Most marketing campaigns fail not from lack of budget or ideas, but because they **say the wrong thing to the wrong people**. The root cause is usually simple: we never really understood who those people are.

A customer persona is the tool that fixes this.

## What a persona is — and is not

**A persona is** a concise description of a typical customer group, built on **real data**: who they are, what problem they face, how they decide.

**A persona is not** a generic demographic table like *"female, 25-35, urban, above-average income"*. That describes millions of people and **will not help you write a single line of ad copy**.

> A good persona answers: "What does this person worry about when they wake up, and which knot does my product untie?"

## 3 free sources for building personas

You do not need expensive market research. Start with what you already have:

1. **Interview past customers** — 5–7 conversations of 20 minutes each is enough to see patterns.
2. **Re-read messages, comments and reviews** — customers describe problems in their own words. This is a goldmine for copywriting.
3. **Existing data** — Google Analytics, page insights, order reports: who buys, what, and when.

## An interview script you can use today

When talking to customers, do not ask *"what do you think of our product?"* (that only earns polite compliments). Ask about **past behavior**:

- What made you start looking for a solution to this problem?
- Before choosing us, what else did you try? Why did it not work?
- While deciding, what made you hesitate most?
- After using it, what changed most noticeably?
- If you recommended it to a friend, how would you describe it?

That last question is especially valuable: **their answer is often your best possible ad headline.**

## A 6-part persona framework

Once you have gathered input, organize it like this:

| Section | Question to answer |
|---|---|
| **Context** | What do they do; what is their life or work like? |
| **Goal** | What are they trying to achieve? |
| **Pain** | What is standing in their way? |
| **Buying barrier** | Why do they hesitate? (price, trust, time...) |
| **Sources** | Who do they trust; where do they read; who do they ask? |
| **Language** | What words do they use to describe the problem? |

**Language** is the most overlooked section, yet the most important for writing content — copy their exact phrasing verbatim.

## Example: from vague to usable

❌ **Vague persona:**
> "Female, 28-40, married, health-conscious, moderate-to-good income."

✅ **Usable persona:**
> "Lan, 34, office worker with two young children. She wants to cook healthy meals for her family but gets home at 6pm with no energy to shop or plan menus. She tried buying vegetables online but quit because delivery was slow and produce arrived wilted. Her biggest worry with a new seller is *'will it actually be fresh, or just good advertising?'* She asks for recommendations in Facebook groups for mothers."

The second persona immediately tells you what to write (emphasize freshness + on-time delivery), where to post (parenting groups), and which barrier to address (with real photos and real reviews).

## 3 common mistakes

1. **Creating too many personas** — start with 1-2 core groups. Too many becomes diluted and unused.
2. **Inventing them from your desk** — a persona not grounded in real data is just your own bias.
3. **Building one then filing it away** — a persona should be reopened every time you write content, choose a channel, or design a landing page.

## Where to start today

If you have no persona yet, take the smallest step: **message your three most recent customers** and ask exactly two questions — *"What made you decide to buy?"* and *"What worried you most beforehand?"*

Those three answers will teach you more than a month of guessing.
